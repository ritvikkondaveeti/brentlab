        ## KIF5A and axonal transport in motor neuron disease

        Kinesin-1, encoded by KIF5A, is the molecular motor that powers long-range transport along axons. Motor neurons depend on this system to maintain function across distances that can span a meter — making them uniquely vulnerable when transport fails.

        Mutations in KIF5A are linked to ALS, hereditary spastic paraplegia, and a rare neonatal neurological disorder. We study how different disease-associated mutations in the same gene affect motor function, neuronal health, and disease course — and what these differences reveal about the mechanisms of selective motor neuron vulnerability.

        Our work combines cell biology, live-cell imaging, and disease models to understand how KIF5A mutations disrupt normal neuronal function and what this means for patients with motor neuron disease.

        ## Nuclear protein dysfunction in ALS

        Many proteins linked to ALS normally function in the nucleus, where they regulate gene expression and maintain cellular health. In disease, these proteins fail to perform their nuclear functions — a process that appears to be a central driver of motor neuron degeneration across multiple ALS genotypes.

        We study the mechanisms by which ALS-associated nuclear proteins lose their normal function, how this failure relates to cellular stress, and why motor neurons appear to be particularly susceptible to these changes.

        Our goal is to identify shared mechanisms across the broad genetic landscape of ALS that could serve as targets for therapeutic intervention.

        ## From clinic to mechanism — understanding variants of uncertain significance

        We see patients with ALS, hereditary spastic paraplegia, Charcot-Marie-Tooth disease, and related disorders in our specialized neuromuscular clinic at UPMC. Many carry genetic variants whose significance is unknown. We work to change that.

        Using a systematic functional characterization approach, we evaluate whether variants found in our patients alter protein behavior in ways consistent with disease. Variants that meet criteria for pathogenicity are reported to patients, their physicians, and national databases.

        We currently study variants in genes including TARDBP, KIF5A, SETX, ERLIN2, and VAPB, with a focus on families where multiple members are affected or where the clinical presentation raises questions that existing data cannot answer.
